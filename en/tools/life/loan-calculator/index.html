Payment Types Explained

Amortized (Equal Payment)

Pay the same amount (principal + interest) each month. Interest portion is higher initially and decreases over time, while principal portion increases. Easy to budget with consistent monthly payments.

Equal Principal

Pay the same principal amount each month, plus interest on the remaining balance. Initial payments are higher but decrease over time. Results in less total interest than amortized loans.

Interest Only (Bullet)

Pay only interest during the loan term, with the full principal due at maturity. Lowest monthly payments but highest total interest cost.

What is a Loan Calculator?

A loan calculator is an essential financial planning tool that helps you estimate monthly payments and total costs for various types of loans. Whether you are planning to buy a home, purchase a car, or take out a personal loan, this calculator provides accurate estimates based on principal amount, interest rate, and loan term. Understanding your loan payments in advance helps you make informed financial decisions and budget effectively.

How to Use

  1. Enter the total loan amount (principal) you wish to borrow
  2. Input the annual interest rate provided by your lender
  3. Select the loan term (repayment period) in months or years
  4. Choose your preferred repayment method (equal principal, equal payment, or bullet)
  5. Click Calculate to see your monthly payments and total interest costs

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: What is the difference between equal principal and equal payment methods?

Equal principal payments mean you pay the same amount of principal each month plus decreasing interest, resulting in decreasing monthly payments. Equal payment (amortization) keeps your monthly payment constant throughout the loan term, with early payments being mostly interest and later payments being mostly principal.

Q: How does the interest rate affect my total loan cost?

Even small differences in interest rates can significantly impact your total loan cost over time. For example, a 1% difference on a 30-year mortgage can mean tens of thousands of dollars in additional interest. Always compare rates from multiple lenders before committing to a loan.

Q: Should I choose a shorter or longer loan term?

Shorter loan terms mean higher monthly payments but less total interest paid. Longer terms offer lower monthly payments but cost more in total interest. Choose based on your monthly budget and financial goals. Consider making extra payments on longer-term loans to save on interest.
