What is Compound Interest?

Compound interest is interest calculated on both the initial principal and the accumulated interest from previous periods. Over time, this creates exponential growth as your interest earns interest.

The Compound Interest Formula

A = P(1 + r/n)^(nt)

A = Final amount, P = Principal, r = Annual interest rate, n = Compounding frequency, t = Time in years

Effect of Compounding Frequency

The more frequently interest is compounded (monthly > quarterly > annually), the more interest you earn. However, the difference becomes more noticeable with higher interest rates.

What is a Compound Interest Calculator?

A compound interest calculator is a financial planning tool that helps you understand how your investments grow over time through the power of compounding. It's useful for planning savings accounts, retirement funds, investment portfolios, and any scenario where your money earns interest on interest.

By adding regular monthly contributions, you can see how consistent investing amplifies the compound effect. The longer your investment period, the more dramatic the compounding benefit becomes.

How to Use

  1. Enter your initial principal amount (e.g., $10,000)
  2. Input the annual interest rate (e.g., 5%)
  3. Select the investment period in years (e.g., 10 years)
  4. Optionally add monthly contributions (e.g., $500/month)
  5. Choose the compounding frequency (annually/semi-annually/quarterly/monthly/daily)
  6. Click "Calculate" to see your projected results

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: What's the difference between simple and compound interest?

Simple interest is calculated only on the principal amount, while compound interest is calculated on the principal plus accumulated interest. Over long periods, compound interest generates significantly higher returns. For example, $100,000 at 5% for 20 years becomes $200,000 with simple interest, but approximately $265,000 with compound interest.

Q: What is the Rule of 72?

The Rule of 72 is a quick way to estimate how long it takes for an investment to double. Divide 72 by the annual interest rate to get the approximate number of years. For example, at 6% interest, 72/6 = 12 years to double your money.

Q: How are monthly contributions calculated?

Monthly contributions are assumed to be made at the beginning of each month, and each contribution earns compound interest for the remaining investment period. Regular contributions are a powerful way to maximize the compound effect over time.
