What is Color Uniformity Testing?

Color Uniformity: Checks if the same color is displayed consistently across the entire screen. If colors appear different at corners or edges, there may be uniformity issues.

Banding: Visible stripes or bands in gradients instead of smooth transitions. This is related to the monitor's color depth and panel quality.

Color Gamut: How wide a range of colors your monitor can accurately display. Check if vibrant reds, greens, and blues are rendered correctly.

Backlight Bleed: Light leaking from edges or corners on a black screen. Best viewed in a dark environment.

What is a Screen Color Test?

A screen color test is a comprehensive display diagnostic tool that evaluates your monitor's color accuracy, uniformity, and overall visual quality. By displaying solid colors and gradient patterns in fullscreen mode, you can identify issues like color banding, backlight bleeding, IPS glow, and color inconsistencies across your screen. This test is invaluable for photographers, designers, video editors, and anyone who needs accurate color reproduction.

How to Use

  1. Click any color or pattern button to enter fullscreen test mode
  2. For solid colors, check that the color appears uniform across the entire screen
  3. Use gradient patterns to detect color banding (visible steps instead of smooth transitions)
  4. Test in a dark room to better spot backlight bleed and IPS glow
  5. Press ESC to exit and compare results with monitor specifications

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: What issues can this test detect?

This test can reveal color uniformity problems (different tints across the screen), backlight bleeding (light leaking at edges on black screens), IPS glow (silvery glow in corners), color banding (visible steps in gradients), and overall color accuracy issues. It is also useful for detecting dead pixels when viewing solid color screens.

Q: What is color banding and why does it happen?

Color banding appears as visible stripes or steps in gradients instead of smooth color transitions. It occurs when a display cannot reproduce enough distinct color levels, often due to limited bit depth (8-bit vs 10-bit panels) or aggressive compression. High-quality monitors with 10-bit panels and good color processing show minimal banding.

Q: Is backlight bleed normal?

Some backlight bleed is common on LED-backlit LCD monitors, especially around edges and corners. Minor bleeding is usually only visible on pure black screens in dark rooms and does not affect normal use. Excessive bleeding that is visible during regular content may indicate a manufacturing defect. OLED displays do not have this issue as each pixel produces its own light.
