UUID Generator

What is UUID Generator?

A UUID (Universally Unique Identifier) Generator creates unique 128-bit identifiers that are virtually guaranteed to be unique across all systems and time. UUIDs are essential in distributed systems, databases, and applications where unique identification is required without central coordination. This tool supports multiple UUID versions including v7 (time-sortable), v4 (random), and v1 (timestamp-based), with options for formatting and bulk generation.

How to Use

  1. Click the refresh button to generate a new UUID instantly, or simply load the page for an automatic first UUID.
  2. Select the UUID version: v7 for time-sortable IDs (recommended for databases), v4 for pure randomness, or v1 for timestamp-based.
  3. Choose the format: lowercase or uppercase, with or without hyphens.
  4. For multiple UUIDs, enter the desired count (1-100) and click "Generate Bulk" to create multiple at once.
  5. Copy individual UUIDs with the copy button, or copy all bulk-generated UUIDs at once.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: What is the difference between UUID v1, v4, and v7?

UUID v1 is based on timestamp and MAC address, making it sortable but potentially exposing device information. UUID v4 is completely random, providing excellent uniqueness but not sortable. UUID v7 (newest standard) combines a Unix timestamp with random data, offering both time-sortability for database performance and strong uniqueness - making it ideal for modern applications.

Q: Can UUIDs ever collide or duplicate?

While theoretically possible, UUID collisions are astronomically unlikely. UUID v4, for example, has 122 random bits, meaning you would need to generate about 2.71 quintillion UUIDs to have a 50% chance of a single collision. In practical terms, UUIDs are considered universally unique and safe to use as primary keys in distributed systems.

Q: Should I use UUIDs with or without hyphens?

Both formats represent the same value. Hyphens improve readability and are the standard format (8-4-4-4-12 pattern). However, removing hyphens saves 4 characters of storage and is sometimes preferred for URLs or when storage space is critical. Most systems accept both formats, so choose based on your specific use case and conventions.
